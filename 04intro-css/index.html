The Decision to Build the Park

The story of Walt Disney's initial Disneyland inspiration centers around a simple afternoon at Griffith Park in Los Angeles, where he was watching his young daughters, Diane and Sharon, ride a merry-go-round on a typical Saturday outing; while seated on a park bench, he envisioned a place where parents and children could enjoy fun activities together, a concept that would later blossom into the magical world of Disneyland.

Key Factors that Shaped Walt's Vision

Walt’s Love of Amusement Parks
Disney was always a fan of amusement parks, especially after taking his daughters to Griffith Park in Los Angeles in the 1930s. While he enjoyed the experience, he was dissatisfied with the quality and cleanliness of the parks. He dreamed of creating a park where families could have a better, more enjoyable experience—a place that was cleaner, more immersive, and specifically designed for children and their parents to enjoy together.
The Success of Disney’s Animated Films
In the 1940s and 1950s, Disney’s animated films like *Snow White and the Seven Dwarfs* and *Cinderella* became huge hits. As his company expanded, Disney realized there was a growing desire for his characters and stories to be experienced in a new way. He wanted to create a physical space where visitors could step into the worlds of his films.
Walt’s Vision of Storytelling and Immersion
Walt Disney was always a visionary storyteller, and he saw Disneyland not just as a theme park but as a living, breathing story. His goal was to create an immersive experience that combined entertainment, education, and fantasy. He wanted the park to be more than a collection of rides—it would be a place where stories were told in an engaging, physical way.
The Influence of Other Theme Parks
Disney was also inspired by other early theme parks, such as the *Coney Island* amusement park in New York and *Tivoli Gardens* in Copenhagen, but he wanted to build something better, more imaginative, and well-organized. He wasn’t just interested in thrill rides but in creating a place that told cohesive, magical stories.
Concept of a "Family Park"
Disney wanted to create a park where both adults and children could have fun. At the time, many amusement parks were seen as places for teens or adults, but Disneyland was intended to be a wholesome, family-friendly environment. His philosophy was to appeal to both the child and the inner child in every adult.
Inspiration from Television
In the 1950s, Walt Disney’s *The Wonderful World of Disney* TV show was becoming hugely popular, and this was a major way he communicated his vision to the public. The show featured segments about the development of Disneyland and helped generate excitement. It also allowed Disney to test ideas with a wider audience and gain public interest.
The Desire for a New Kind of Business
Disney was always thinking about new business ventures. In addition to the film studio and television, Disneyland represented a new kind of creative business: a fully integrated entertainment experience. The park would not just generate revenue through ticket sales but also through merchandise, food, and even hotels.

Around 1952, Walt Disney started seriously thinking about how to make his dream a reality. He consulted with architects, designers, and engineers, and after scouting various locations, he decided on a 160-acre plot in Anaheim, California. The location was chosen for its proximity to Los Angeles, ease of access, and affordable land compared to other parts of the city.

Disney’s vision was clear: a place where "fantasy" and "reality" could come together, a park where "adults and children could enjoy themselves together." His innovative ideas, creativity, and relentless pursuit of perfection led to the creation of Disneyland, which opened on July 17, 1955. It was an immediate success, and it forever changed the landscape of theme parks and entertainment worldwide.
